**I don’t trust the first diagram.**

It’s usually a lie.

Not an evil lie. A premature one. Too clean, too linear, too eager to pretend the thinking went from A to B without the detour through confusion and one or two bad ideas wearing professional clothes.

I started drawing on computers with AutoCAD R10 in the early 90s, so I’m not anti-diagram. I’m anti diagram-as-performance.

I’ve also spent an unnecessary number of hours in Visio, fighting the canvas harder than the problem, trying to force something real into a box-and-arrow story that looked presentable and quietly deleted everything inconvenient.

![etch-ai-sketch-using-a-council-to-design-at-velocity-copilot-v1-2026-03-18-042801.png](attachment:ae01fc76-bb8c-4bce-a3d8-28b4b332e709:etch-ai-sketch-using-a-council-to-design-at-velocity-copilot-v1-2026-03-18-042801.png)

*Copilot V1* 

Then a few months ago, an LLM coughed up something I didn’t ask for. I asked what it was and got a new rabbit hole: a Mermaid diagram, rendered natively in Microsoft Loop.

The pivot for me wasn’t the syntax. It was the economics. When diagrams get cheap enough to draft and revise the way we revise prose, they stop being end-of-project artifacts and start being thinking tools.

That’s where the metric came from: ROY, Return on Your Words. The question isn’t whether a picture is worth 1,000 words. The question is whether the words you spent bought real understanding, or just another paragraph someone skims and forgets.

And once you can generate structure fast, you can also see how often the first diagram lies. It shows the happy path. The later versions show what the work actually looks like: loops, forks, cleanup, revision, and the moment you realize the clever version isn’t the useful one.

*Claude V2*

I pushed the idea further by running the same brief through a Council of AIs and comparing outputs. Not because any one model is right, but because differences make the structure visible.

Six models. Same prompt. Radically different instincts about what honesty looks like in a flowchart.
