<h2 id="prompts">Prompts in the Wild</h2>
<h3 style="font-family: var(--font-body); font-weight: 600; font-size: 1rem; text-transform: none; letter-spacing: 0; color: var(--color-muted); margin-top: -0.5rem; margin-bottom: 1.5rem;">Velocity, iteration, and vibe over premature prompt perfection</h3>

<p>
  One of the points of this experiment is that the prompts were <strong>not</strong> highly polished.
</p>
<p>
  They were not lab-grade.<br />
  They were not deeply pre-scripted.<br />
  They were not engineered like final-form production assets.
</p>
<p>
  Most of them started as live dictation — quick, directional, natural-language prompts
  spoken in the moment while the idea was still moving.
</p>
<p>That matters.</p>
<p>
  If every prompt had to be honed, polished, and weaponized before use, the process would
  lose much of what made it valuable. The friction would rise. The mental cost would rise.
  The velocity would drop. At some point the tooling tax is back — just wearing a different
  costume. May as well open Visio.
</p>

<div class="pull-quote">
  <p>Prompt precision has a cost. Sometimes that cost is worth paying. Sometimes it is not.</p>
</div>

<p>
  In exploratory work — especially when using Mermaid to sketch, iterate, and surface
  structure — the priority is forward motion, not perfect inputs.
</p>
<p>
  These prompts were lightly edited for readability only. Grammar corrected, duplicate
  phrasing from live dictation removed, word overhead trimmed. The original shape and
  intent were preserved intentionally.
</p>
<p>
  That is the point.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Prompting in practice is often fast, natural, and iterative.</strong>
</p>

  <details class="code-drop">

    <pre class="artifact-copy">Thinking like a professor of business and investment, what is a realistic

There is an old adage that a picture is worth 1,000 words. If a diagram
can be generated from words, what would be a good ROY — return on your
words? If it takes more words to generate the picture than the picture
returns in meaning, understanding, or compression, the investment may be
poor. But if a picture created from words lands with more meaning than the
words it took to create it, that seems valuable.

What ratio of words to meaning, understanding, and structure makes this a
good investment?</pre>

What are the steps in creating a LinkedIn post about visual value, visual

Generating a diagram from words involves revision. Revision involves forks

rather than a straight one, with slight visual flair: restrained color
variation, selective arrow styling, subtle hierarchy, and conservative
emoji use.

Enough to add clarity and character. Not enough to become noise.</pre>

</p>
