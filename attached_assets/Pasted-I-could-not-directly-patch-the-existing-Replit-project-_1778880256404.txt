## v0.4 — Why I Built a Council of AIs

Section anchor:

#council-origin

Add this body copy:

By the time I had a working draft of the first diagram, I had already run the same brief through several AI platforms.
That was not the original plan.
It started as impatience.
I asked one model for a Mermaid diagram of the ideation process behind a LinkedIn post. It came back too clean. Too linear. Too confident. It looked like a happy path, and I have spent enough time around real processes to know that happy paths are a form of fiction.
So I asked another model.
Then another.
At some point I stopped pretending this was troubleshooting and admitted it was a method.
What I was running was a Council.
Not counsel, though that is what it eventually produced. A council is the body. Counsel is the output. When you convene a council, you are not looking for one correct answer. You are manufacturing perspective. You are creating the conditions under which disagreement can surface structure that no single voice would have seen on its own.
That distinction matters more than it sounds.
One AI can give you an answer.
A Council gives you variance.
And variance is where the useful thinking hides.

4. Add subsection: Co-opetition

Add this section immediately after the Council intro.

Heading:

### Co-opetition: Why the Models Compete and Cooperate

Body:

The Council works because the models are similar enough to compete, but different enough to matter.
That is the co-opetition layer.
Each model receives the same brief and produces its own answer independently. In that phase, the models compete. They reveal their instincts, their shortcuts, their defaults, and their blind spots.
Then the human work begins.
I compare the outputs. I look for divergence, not just agreement. One model treats the process like a straight pipeline. Another introduces a feedback loop. Another surfaces a decision point. Another reduces the whole thing so aggressively that I have to decide whether it found the essence or amputated the nuance.
That disagreement is not noise.
That disagreement is the signal.
The models compete in the first pass.
They cooperate in the synthesis.
That is the part people miss when they talk about using “an AI” as if all models are interchangeable. They are not. Even when they can technically perform the same task, they do not frame the task the same way. They do not compress the same details. They do not notice the same absences. They do not lie in the same direction.
For diagramming, that matters.
A diagram is not just an answer. It is a structural interpretation. It decides what exists, what connects, what loops, what branches, what gets hidden, and what gets treated as linear because linear is easier to draw.
A single model may produce a useful diagram, but it also hides its assumptions inside the diagram.
You do not see those assumptions clearly until another model makes different ones.

5. Add subsection: Manual Council loop

Heading:

### The Manual Council Loop

Body:

What I was doing in early 2026 was crude, but effective.
I wrote the brief. The same brief, as faithfully as I could manage. I ran it through ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Perplexity, Gemini, Notion, and eventually Replit. I collected the outputs. I read them side by side. I looked for where they diverged.
Then I stole the useful parts without apology.
A loop from one.
A label from another.
A phase structure from a third.
A cleaner framing from a fourth.
Then I synthesized the version that none of them produced alone.
The loop was simple:
1. Fan out — same brief, independent runs, no cross-contamination.
2. Compare — side by side, looking for divergence instead of consensus.
3. Adjudicate — decide which differences revealed something true and which were just noise.
4. Synthesize — build the hybrid that none of the originals could have been.
That is not automation.
That is judgment.
And for a certain class of problem, especially the kind where the shape of the answer matters as much as the answer itself, judgment is the whole game.

6. Add subsection: Platforms are beginning to automate this

Heading:

### What the Platforms Are Beginning to Automate

Body:

Here is the part that has aged interestingly since this experiment started.
The premium AI market is beginning to productize pieces of the Council pattern.
Perplexity, in particular, has moved toward multi-model comparison in higher-tier offerings: ask once, send the question across multiple models, compare model A, model B, model C, and potentially review a synthesized answer.
That is useful.
It is also not exactly what I was doing.
The productized version optimizes for convenience, speed, and higher-confidence answers. Ask once. Compare quickly. Synthesize faster.
My manual version had a different purpose.
I wanted to study the disagreement before smoothing it into a final answer.
Consensus is useful when you need an answer.
Disagreement is useful when you need to understand the shape of the problem.
That is why the crude manual version still matters. It forces the human operator to see the assumptions before the synthesis hides them.

7. Add subsection: Council membership

Heading:

### What Each Council Member Brought to the Room

Body:

The Council was not random.
Each participant brought a different kind of pressure to the work.
ChatGPT was the workbench: fast ideation, scaffolding, synthesis, and the place where the most context had already accumulated.
Claude was the structural analyst: strong at narrative architecture, critique, and pushing back when the shape of the argument was too convenient.
Copilot was the integrationist: practical, Microsoft-native, and unusually attentive to renderer-level configuration and operational fit.
Perplexity was the precision lens: compact framing, external signal, and a bias toward grounding.
Gemini was the reduction pressure: sometimes too restrained, sometimes useful precisely because it forced the question of whether simplicity was insight or loss.
Notion was the documentarian: the place where lineage, drafts, artifacts, and synthesis could be preserved.
Replit arrived late and under-briefed, which was my fault as much as the tool’s. It deserves its own later treatment because running an unfamiliar builder inside a high-context experiment teaches a different lesson.
Mermaid.ai was the specialist that should have been the obvious winner, but the workflow exposed a sequencing problem: it rendered too early. For this experiment, the ability to hold context before drawing mattered more than raw Mermaid fluency.
The Council was not assembled to find a mascot.
It was assembled to create a field of perspectives wide enough that the synthesis could be honest rather than merely confident.

8. Add subsection: What the Council produced

Heading:

### What No Single Model Produced Alone

Body:

The honest answer is that no single Council submission was better than the synthesized result.
Not one.
The synthesis borrowed structure from Copilot, framing from Claude, loop logic from ChatGPT, business clarity from Perplexity, reduction pressure from Gemini, and recordkeeping from Notion.
The winning diagram did not exist in any single output.
It emerged from comparing all of them.
That is the argument for Councils as a method, not just as a curiosity. If you are working on a problem where structure matters, architecture, process design, communication strategy, diagramming, product framing, anything where shape determines clarity, a Council run will surface something a single-model run will not.
The manual overhead is real.
The discipline required is real.
The payoff, when the problem warrants it, is also real.
One AI can help you move faster.
A Council can help you see better.
And when the work is diagramming, seeing better is the whole point.
