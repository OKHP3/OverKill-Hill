The First Diagram
Is Usually a Liar

A picture is not automatically worth 1,000 words. The real metric is ROY — Return on Your Words: the understanding extracted per word invested. What I learned asking six AIs to draw my thinking — and why the first diagram always lies.

Jamie Hill OverKill Hill P³™ March 2025 ~8 min read
🔧 Protoform — v0.1 This is the functional beta. The architecture is sound; the full Snyder Cut — scoring rubrics, round-by-round breakdowns, complete diagram evolution, the PowerPoint layer, and the Council of AIs methodology as a repeatable framework — is coming. This page will update in place when it does.

I started drawing on a computer in 1991.

AutoCAD 10. Black screen. Command line. No drag-and-drop, no shape libraries — just coordinates, precision, and the full weight of engineering intent behind every line. Back then, turning a thought into a visual required discipline, patience, and a working knowledge of the tool that stood between your idea and its expression.

Thirty-three years later, I can describe a process in plain language and watch an AI render it as a structured diagram in seconds.

The tool changed. The underlying question didn't.

Is the picture actually worth what it cost to make it?

That question has followed me across every medium I've worked in: house plans, network diagrams, process flows, mind maps, org charts, UML schemas, crow's foot ERDs. If an idea could be visualized, I've stared at a blank canvas trying to figure out how to start it.

And for a long time — longer than I'd like to admit — the blank canvas won a few rounds before I did.

Not because the ideas were weak. Because the translation was expensive. You had to know the tool. You had to fight the toolbar. You had to make the picture behave before it could make your idea understood.

I cannot count the hours I spent staring at the Microsoft Visio UI thinking: "How in the hell am I going to draw this?"

That friction wasn't just annoying. It was a tax. A Manual Labor Tax on every idea that deserved a visual but got a paragraph instead — because the paragraph was faster, and fast usually wins.

Then, about eight months ago, ChatGPT coughed up a diagram I didn't ask for.

I was describing a process. The model responded with structured syntax I didn't recognize. I typed, essentially: "What the hell is that?"

It replied: "A Mermaid diagram."

Mermaid is a text-based diagramming language. You describe what you want in plain language. An LLM converts it into syntax. The syntax renders as a diagram. No Figma. No Canva. No toolbar archaeology. No snap-to-grid negotiations with your own ideas.

Just words — becoming structure.

I asked Copilot to generate one. Discovered that Microsoft Loop renders Mermaid natively. Went down the rabbit hole that people like me go down — and never fully came back up.

The Manual Labor Tax had just been repealed.

ROY: Return on Your Words

Which forced a question I hadn't seriously considered before: what is the actual return on a word? Not the word count. Not the reading time. The return — the understanding extracted per word invested.

The ROY Formula

Clarity Delivered ÷ Words Required to Deliver It

Return on Your Words = understanding created ÷ words invested

A 47-bullet Slack message? Poor ROY. A three-paragraph "quick summary" that still requires a meeting to decode? Negative ROY. A diagram generated from 50 words of plain description that a team grasps in five seconds and moves on from with alignment? Potentially extraordinary ROY.

But — and this is the part most "visuals are better" takes skip — not every picture earns its words.

A muddy diagram is worse than a clear paragraph. A happy-path flowchart that pretends thinking is linear is a lie dressed up as structure. Decorative visuals with a superiority complex aren't communication. They're performance.

That's why the first diagram is usually a liar. Too clean. Too linear. Too confident. It edits out the confusion that actually mattered. Real thinking loops. It forks. It backtracks. It occasionally discovers that the clever version was performing while the useful version was quietly doing the actual work.

Did the visual remove enough confusion to justify the effort it took to make it? That's the ROY test.

The Proof: A Diagram of This Post's Own Creation

So I ran an experiment. A fairly ridiculous one. Very on-brand.

Instead of writing a post about visual ROI and moving on, I gave the same brief to six AI platforms simultaneously — Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, Perplexity, and Notion AI — and asked each to generate both the article and a Mermaid diagram depicting the actual ideation process behind writing the post itself.

The recursive part: the diagram wasn't illustrating some external concept. The diagram was the concept. The subject of the post was the method. The method was the proof.

The Council of AIs

I collected all six results. Fed them back to the group. Asked for evaluation, scoring, and synthesis. Then asked for a second round — a V2 — informed by what each model learned from seeing the others' work.

What I was running, without initially naming it, was a Council of AIs.

Not automation. Not a pipeline. A deliberative body — six distinct voices, the same question, a human orchestrator weighing the results and directing the synthesis. Each model had different strengths. No single one won everything. The best result didn't exist until the council produced it together.

Council of AIs — Round 1 & 2

How the evaluation worked

Same brief given to all six members simultaneously. Outputs collected. All results fed back to the group for critique and scoring. Then a second round — V2 — informed by what each model learned from seeing the others' work.

ChatGPT Claude ✦ V2 Copilot ✦ V1 Gemini Perplexity Notion AI

That's also the ROY lesson applied to AI itself. One prompt to one model is the 47-bullet Slack message. A council is the diagram. More investment upfront. Significantly higher return on what you extract.

The Thesis, Standing on Its Own

Words have a return. That return is measured in understanding, not volume.

A picture generated from 50 well-chosen words — genuinely clearer, faster, and more universally grasped than the prose it replaced — has just delivered a multiplier on your words' investment that no paragraph can match.

The question worth sitting with before your next explanation, your next documentation, your next "let me walk you through this":

When is meaning best delivered as words — and when is it better delivered as the visual shape those words can bring into existence?

This is the protoform. The council is still in session.
